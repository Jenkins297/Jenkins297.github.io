What is the nanobubble mystery?

Introduction

Experiments show us that very small stable bubbles, known as nanobubbles, can be present on surfaces despite well founded theoretical considerations that predict otherwise.

Physicists have long debated whether they really exists.

Due to the high Laplace pressure inside the bubbles that drives the gas into the liquid, the lifetime of surface nanobubbles is expected to be on the order of microseconds, rather than the many hours or even days observed experimentally.

Bubbles (Young–Laplace + Henry's law)

Young-Laplace equation

The increase in pressure inside a spherical bubble due to the surface tension is described by the Young–Laplace equation, which for a spherical object is given by

\[ \Delta p = \frac{2 \gamma}{R} \]

Where \( \gamma \) is the surface tension of the bubble and \( R \) is the radius of curvature of the bubble.

Henry's law

In chemistry, Henry's law is a gas law that states that the amount of dissolved gas is proportional to its partial pressure in the gas phase.

The Young–Laplace + Henry feedback cycle

This positive feedback cycle can rapidly lead to the dissolution and disappearance of small bubbles.

  • 01. Pressure

    The pressure within a bubble is higher than the surroundings.

  • 02. Solubility

    The elevation in pressure results in an increase in solubility of the gases that make up the bubble in the surrounding liquid.

  • 03. Diffusion

    Gas leaves the bubble by diffusion and is solvated in the liquid in order to establish equilibrium.

  • 04. Size

    The loss of gas from the bubble leads to a decrease in bubble size and an increase in bubble pressure.

Bubble lifetime

Theoretical predictions

For micron sized bubbles the theory predicts that they should disappear within tens of milliseconds, and that the attachment of the bubble to a surface should only have a small effect on the bubble lifetime.

Surface nanobubbles

Experimental evidence

The most direct evidence for the existence of these exceptionally stable nanobubbles is provided by AFM images.

Because of the solid basis on which the classical theories are built, early reports of the existence of stable nanobubbles at surfaces were greeted with scepticism.

Figure:
AFM tapping mode images of nanobubbles on HOPG in water.
Image size 10 μm ⨯ 10 μm[1].

Experimental challenges

  • Deformability

    Imaging artifacts caused by the deformability of nanobubbles.

  • Contamination

    Nanobubble–AFM tip interactions could be affected by contaminants on the surface or at the gas–liquid interface.

  • AFM tip

    It is difficult to determine the tip profile precisely and accurately in a non-destructive manner.

Pinned contact line

The empirically verified pinned contact line of surface nanobubbles is crucial for long nanobubble lifetimes.

\[ \Delta p = \frac{2 \gamma}{R} = \frac{2 \gamma}{R_b} \text{sin}\ \theta \]

For surface nanobubbles (\( \theta \) < 90°), the internal pressure thus decreases as \( \theta \) decreases, providing a negative feedback in the dissolution process.

Figure:
Sketch of a liquid layer of thickness \( \ell \) in contact with a solid (left). On the right a further enlargement of one nanobubble is shown.[2].

[2] Joost H. Weijs and Detlef Lohse. Why surface nanobubbles live for hours. Phys. Rev. Lett., 110:054501, Jan 2013.

Limited far field diffusion

The characteristic time scale for diffusion through the water layer should be modified by using \( \ell \) as the relevant length scale instead of the bubble radius \( R \).

\[ \tau = \frac{\ell^2}{D} \sim 10^5\ \text{s} \approx 28\ \text{hours} \]

Using typical parameters that apply to experiments on surface nanobubbles ( \( \ell \) = 10-2 m and \( D \) = 10-9 m2/s), we recover values similar to the lifetimes obtained in experiments.

The extraordinarily long lifetimes of surface nanobubbles.

mystery solved (mostly)

The theory relies only on well-established, and proven concepts such as diffusion and Henry’s law. Furthermore, the theory only uses confirmed nanobubble properties, namely the pinned contact line of nanobubbles and the fact that nanobubbles exist in relatively high coverage fractions at the liquid–solid interface.

The correct determination of the radius of curvature as well as the unequivocal assignment of the nanoscale features to gas-filled cavities remain central objectives still to be solved.

Holger Schönherr et al (2016)
